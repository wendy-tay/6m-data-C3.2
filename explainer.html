Part 1 — Scoring the model

Every prediction has an error.

A model never nails the price exactly. MAE (Mean Absolute Error) is the average gap between predicted and actual price across many flats. Move the slider to see how a model that's "tighter" lowers the average dollar error.

Scenario: Five real flats sold last month. Your model guessed each one. How far off was it, on average? That single dollar number is your model's report card.

Part 2 — More relevant information

Give the model more to look at.

Your L05 model only knew size, lease year, and floor. But price also depends on what kind of flat it is and where it sits. Toggle features on and watch the error bar shrink.

Scenario: A 5-room in Bishan and a 2-room in Yishun can be the same size. Without "flat type" and "town", the model treats them as identical. Add those clues and it gets sharper.

Part 3 — Choosing a model honestly

Learning, not memorising.

A fancier model can fit the training flats almost perfectly — but flop on new ones. We catch this by comparing error on flats it trained on vs flats it has never seen. Slide model complexity and watch the two lines.

Scenario: A student who memorises past exam papers scores 100% on those papers but fails new questions. A model that "memorises" training flats does the same. The gap between the two lines is the warning sign.
